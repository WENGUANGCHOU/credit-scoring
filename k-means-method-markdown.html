The dataset is a standard table with countries and several indicators shown and described below:

Child mortality refers to the number of deaths of children under 5 years of age per 1,000 live births in a given population. Source: World Health Organization (WHO) - Child Mortality

Exports per capita represents the total value of goods and services exported by a country divided by the population of that country. Source: International Monetary Fund (IMF) - Exports of goods and services (BoP, current US$)

Health spending per capita refers to the total expenditure on health (including both public and private spending) divided by the population of a country. Source: World Bank - Current health expenditure per capita (current US$)

Imports per capita represents the total value of goods and services imported by a country divided by the population of that country. Source: International Monetary Fund (IMF) - Imports of goods and services (BoP, current US$)

Income per person, also known as per capita income, represents the average income earned by individuals in a country, calculated by dividing the total income by the population. Source: World Bank - GNI per capita, Atlas method (current US$)

Inflation is a measure of the rate of increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over a specific period of time. It indicates the decrease in purchasing power of a currency. Source: International Monetary Fund (IMF) - Inflation, consumer prices (annual %)

Life expectancy refers to the average number of years a person is expected to live, based on current age-specific mortality rates. Source: World Health Organization (WHO) - Life expectancy

Total Fertility Rate (TFR) represents the average number of children that would be born to a woman over her lifetime if she were to experience the current age-specific fertility rates throughout her reproductive years. Source: World Bank - Total fertility rate (births per woman)

GDP per capita represents the total Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of a country divided by its population. It provides a measure of the average economic output per person in a country. Source: World Bank - GDP per capita, PPP (current international $)

As explained in class, the effectiveness of k-means clustering is greatly influenced by the scaling of variables, making it essential to have variables with commensurate units. Additionally, if the distribution of the variables being analyzed is skewed, k-means clustering may yield unfavorable outcomes.

We will proceed to test the skewness of the selected variables.

After calculating the skewness scores for each variable, the results are as follows:

Child mortality: Skewness score = 1.43771 Income: Skewness score = 2.211386 Life expectancy: Skewness score = -0.9622522

These skewness scores provide insights into the distributional characteristics of the variables. A positive skewness score indicates a longer tail on the right side of the distribution, while a negative skewness score indicates a longer tail on the left side. A skewness score close to zero suggests a relatively symmetric distribution.

Based on the scores obtained, income appears to have a relatively high positive skewness, while life expectancy has a negative skewness. Child mortality has a skewness scores close to 1, indicating a slightly right-skewed distribution.

Considering these scores, it may be advisable to explore potential data transformations for variables with notable skewness to attain a more symmetric distribution prior to undertaking the k-means clustering analysis.
